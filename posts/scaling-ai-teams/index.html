Building and Scaling AI Research Teams in Biotech

October 28, 2025
Rohola Zandie
6 min read
Leadership Team Building AI Research Biotech

Building a successful AI research team in the biotech industry presents unique challenges that go far beyond traditional software development or even conventional AI applications. Having co-founded Absentia Labs and scaled our research team from the ground up, I've learned valuable lessons about what it takes to create a high-performing, innovative AI research organization in the biotechnology space.

The Unique Landscape of AI in Biotech

Unlike other AI applications, biotech AI research sits at the intersection of multiple complex domains. Your team members need to understand not just machine learning algorithms and software engineering, but also biology, chemistry, pharmacology, and often regulatory requirements. This interdisciplinary nature makes team building both challenging and critical to success.

The stakes are also different in biotech. While a recommendation algorithm might affect user engagement, AI decisions in drug discovery could ultimately impact patient lives. This reality shapes everything from the types of people you hire to the culture you build around precision, validation, and ethical responsibility.

Building the Foundation: Core Team Structure

When building an AI research team for biotech, I've found that certain roles and skill combinations are essential from the early stages:

The AI-Biology Bridge Builders

The most valuable team members are those who can speak both languages fluently. These are researchers with strong computational backgrounds who have also worked extensively with biological data, or biologists who have developed deep technical skills in machine learning. These bridge builders become your translators, helping pure computational researchers understand biological constraints and helping domain experts leverage AI capabilities.

Platform Engineers with Domain Awareness

Standard software engineering practices don't always translate directly to biotech AI. You need engineers who understand the unique requirements of handling biological data—its scale, noise characteristics, regulatory considerations, and the need for reproducibility. These platform engineers build the infrastructure that enables researchers to work efficiently while maintaining the rigor required in life sciences.

Translational Researchers

Having team members who understand the pathway from computational insights to real-world applications is crucial. These researchers help ensure that your AI work remains grounded in practical applications and regulatory realities.

Hiring Strategies That Work

Look for Learning Velocity Over Perfect Fit

Given the interdisciplinary nature of the work, it's rare to find candidates who have deep expertise in all relevant areas. Instead, I focus on hiring people with demonstrated ability to quickly master new domains. Look for candidates who have successfully made transitions between fields or who have contributed to projects outside their formal training.

Value Intellectual Humility

The complexity of biological systems means that even the most sophisticated AI models can fail in unexpected ways. The best team members are those who approach problems with intellectual humility—they're confident in their abilities but also deeply aware of what they don't know and eager to learn from failures.

Assess Problem-Solving Over Specific Technical Skills

While technical competency is important, the rapidly evolving nature of both AI and biotech means that specific technical skills can become outdated quickly. Focus on candidates who demonstrate strong problem-solving abilities, creative thinking, and the ability to break down complex problems into manageable components.

"In biotech AI, the ability to navigate uncertainty and make progress despite incomplete information is often more valuable than expertise in any specific technique or algorithm."

Fostering Innovation and Collaboration

Create Cross-Functional Project Teams

Some of our most successful projects have come from small, cross-functional teams that include both computational and domain experts working closely together. This structure encourages knowledge transfer and ensures that technical solutions are grounded in biological reality.

Encourage Controlled Risk-Taking

Innovation requires experimentation, and experimentation means some projects will fail. Create an environment where intelligent risks are rewarded even when they don't pan out, while maintaining the rigor necessary for biotech applications.

Invest in Continuous Learning

The pace of advancement in both AI and biotech means that continuous learning isn't optional—it's essential. We allocate significant time and resources for team members to attend conferences, take courses, and explore new approaches. This investment pays dividends in terms of both individual growth and team capabilities.

Managing the Technical-Business Balance

One of the biggest challenges in scaling AI research teams in biotech is balancing cutting-edge research with business objectives. Unlike purely academic research, your work needs to ultimately contribute to practical applications that can advance through development pipelines.

Set Clear Success Metrics

Establish metrics that bridge technical achievement and business value. This might include biological validation rates for computational predictions, time-to-insight for new hypotheses, or the quality of target identification for downstream development.

Maintain Strong Industry Connections

Keep your team connected to both the academic research community and industry practitioners. This dual connection helps ensure your research remains both scientifically rigorous and practically relevant.

Scaling Challenges and Solutions

As your team grows, several challenges become pronounced:

Maintaining Research Quality

Implement robust peer review processes and maintain high standards for experimental validation. The temptation to move quickly can lead to shortcuts that ultimately slow progress.

Knowledge Management

In a rapidly growing team working on complex problems, knowledge management becomes critical. Invest in systems and processes that capture not just what was done, but why decisions were made and what was learned from failures.

Preserving Culture

The collaborative, learning-oriented culture that works well in small teams can be challenging to maintain as you scale. Be intentional about culture preservation through hiring, onboarding, and team structures.

Building for the Future

As AI continues to evolve and its applications in biotech expand, the teams that will be most successful are those that can adapt quickly while maintaining their core strengths. This means:

  • Building diverse perspectives: Include team members from different backgrounds, disciplines, and career stages
  • Staying connected to the broader ecosystem: Maintain relationships with academic institutions, other biotech companies, and technology partners
  • Investing in foundational capabilities: Focus on building strong foundations in data infrastructure, experimental design, and validation methodologies
  • Preparing for regulatory evolution: Stay ahead of evolving regulatory frameworks for AI in healthcare

Conclusion

Building and scaling an AI research team in biotech is one of the most challenging and rewarding endeavors in technology today. The opportunity to work at the intersection of cutting-edge computational methods and life-changing therapeutic applications attracts some of the most talented and motivated researchers in the world.

Success requires more than just hiring smart people—it demands creating an environment where interdisciplinary collaboration thrives, where intellectual rigor is paired with practical focus, and where the ultimate goal of improving human health drives every decision.

The teams that master this balance will be the ones that define the future of AI in biotechnology, turning the promise of computational drug discovery into reality for patients worldwide.
